0From Discrete to Continuous Distributions

A bridge lesson preceding §1 (Continuous Random Variables). The numbers stop being whole. Probabilities stop being sums and start being areas. By the end of this hour you will know why.

0.1Looking back: discrete distributions

So far, every random variable $X$ in this course has been discrete: it could only land on isolated values. The number of sixes in five dice rolls is one of $\{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$. Nothing in between.

That made probabilities easy to reason about. To find $P(2 \le X \le 4)$ we just summed three numbers:

$$P(2 \le X \le 4) \;=\; P(X{=}2) + P(X{=}3) + P(X{=}4).$$

Each term was a height in a histogram. Each term was a probability you could read off a bar. The example below lets you revisit the three discrete distributions you have studied.

Discrete · what you know
  • $X$ takes isolated values $x_1, x_2, \ldots$
  • $P(X = x_i)$ is a real number you can list in a table
  • Probabilities add: $P(X \le k) = \sum_{i \le k} P(X = x_i)$
  • Examples: binomial, hypergeometric, geometric
Continuous · today
  • $X$ takes any real value in some interval
  • $P(X = a) = 0$ — yes, really, zero
  • Probabilities are areas, not sums
  • Examples: heights, weights, waiting times
Note The shift from "isolated values" to "any real number" is the whole point of today's lesson. Everything that follows in §1 — the bell curve, the de Moivre–Laplace approximation, $z$-scores — is built on it.

0.2From measurements to a density curve

Imagine we measure the body height of every student at our Kanti — and then a thousand more like it. We collect a heap of numbers like 167.2, 172.8, 168.0, 181.4, 159.6, …

Group those numbers into bins, draw a histogram. As we collect more data and use narrower bins, the histogram stops looking like a histogram and starts looking like a curve.

Two things to notice as you push the sliders:

  1. The shape stabilises. Small samples give jagged histograms; large samples settle into something smooth and symmetric.
  2. The bars shrink in height. When you halve the bin width, each bar covers half as many measurements. The height is adjusted so that the area of every bar equals its share of the data — keeping the total area at exactly $1$.
Definition 0.1 · probability density (informal) The smooth limit curve we see emerging is called the probability density of the random variable, written $f(x)$. Its graph is the histogram with infinitely thin bars, scaled so that the total area beneath equals $1$.

0.3Why $P(X = 170\,\text{cm}) = 0$

Pick a student at random from a very large population. What is the probability that her height is exactly $170\,\text{cm}$? Not $170.0$. Not $170.00$. Exactly $170$, with infinitely many zeros after the decimal point.

However many decimal places we demand, only a vanishingly small slice of the population can match all of them. As we keep zooming in on "exactly $170$", the slice we are asking about gets thinner — and thinner, and thinner — until its width is zero. And a strip of zero width has zero area.

Example 0.3.Watch the strip shrink to zero width.
strip width: 4.0 cm · area: ≈ 0.20
Key fact For a continuous random variable, single points carry no probability. Probability lives on intervals, and an interval has probability equal to the area under the density curve $f$ above it.

This is the deepest break with the discrete world. There, every value came pre-packaged with its own probability, ready to be summed. Here, individual values are like individual points on a number line: they exist, but they have no length.

0.4An old friend grows a new shape

§0.2 showed where density curves come from. It did not show why one particular curve — the bell — keeps appearing everywhere from cherry weights to recruit heights to IQ scores. Here is a clue. Take a binomial distribution $B(n, p)$ — a coin tossed $n$ times, the count of heads. Watch what happens to its histogram as $n$ grows.

Push $n$ to $50$, $80$, $120$. Toggle Show bell and the curve glides over the bars almost perfectly. Slide $p$ away from $\tfrac{1}{2}$: the histogram tilts, and so does the bell. The match remains striking as long as $n$ is large enough.

Theorem 0.1 · de Moivre–Laplace (preview) For large $n$, the binomial distribution $B(n, p)$ is well approximated by a continuous bell-shaped density with mean $\mu = np$ and standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)}$. This bell is the normal distribution, which is the headline act of §1 and §2 of the script.
Note Why does the bell appear here at all? Each binomial outcome is a sum of many independent coin flips. The Central Limit Theorem says sums of many small independent influences always tend towards a bell — which is why heights, measurement errors and so much else of nature look the way they do.

0.5Probability is area

If single points carry no probability, where does probability actually live? On intervals — and the rule is beautifully simple.

$$P(a \le X \le b) \;=\; \text{area under } f(x) \text{ from } a \text{ to } b.$$

Two facts to lock in:

  • Move both handles to the same spot. The shaded strip vanishes. The probability is $0$. (We saw this in §0.3.)
  • Drag them to the extremes. The full area under the curve is $1$ — the random variable lands somewhere, with certainty.
Outlook · the reason for our next chapter Reading areas off a graph by eye is fine for intuition. But the bell curve has no friendly antiderivative we can write down. To compute these areas exactly we need a precise tool for "area under a curve" — a tool called the integral. That is what we will build in the next analysis chapter, and what the formula $\;F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t)\,dt\;$ in Definition 1.1 of the script will mean.

0.6Exercises

  1. For each random variable, decide whether it is discrete or continuous. Tap the boxes that you think are continuous.
    Continuous: (b), (c), (e). Time, mass and rainfall can take any real value in some interval. The other two are counts — they jump from one whole number to the next and so are discrete.
  2. Why is $P(X = 170\,\text{cm}) = 0$ for a continuous random variable $X$ — but $P(X = 3) = \tfrac{1}{6}$ when $X$ is the result of a single die roll? Write your reasoning in one or two sentences.
    For a die roll, the value $3$ is one of six equally likely outcomes — it carries one sixth of the total probability mass. For a continuous height, the value $170$ is one of uncountably many possible real numbers in the relevant interval. The "strip" of probability above a single point has zero width, so it has zero area — and therefore zero probability. Probability for a continuous variable lives on intervals, not points.
  3. Sketch (on paper) a possible density curve for the lifetime in years of a particular brand of light bulb. Mark on your sketch which area corresponds to the probability that a bulb lasts between $1$ and $2$ years.
    A reasonable sketch: a curve starting at $f(0) = 0$, rising quickly to a peak somewhere around $0.5$–$1$ year, then falling off slowly towards the right and approaching the $x$-axis as $x \to \infty$. The total area under the curve must be $1$. The probability $P(1 \le X \le 2)$ is the shaded slice between the vertical lines $x=1$ and $x=2$, bounded above by the curve and below by the $x$-axis.
  4. From Example 0.5, drag the handles to $a = -1$ and $b = 1$ on the standard normal curve. Read off the value of $P(-1 \le X \le 1)$ to two decimal places and type it here:
    You should read approximately $\mathbf{0.68}$. This is the famous "$68\%$ rule": about $68\%$ of a normal distribution lies within one standard deviation of the mean. Theorem $1.1$ and the table on the next page of the script will pin this down to four decimal places.
  5. For a continuous random variable $X$, decide whether each statement is true or false.
    Only the third statement is true. The first two confuse the density value $f(x)$ with a probability — $f(x)$ is a density (probability per unit length), not a probability itself. The fourth statement should be $P(X \le 170) + P(X \ge 170) = 1$, since for any $a$, $P(X = a) = 0$ and the two events together cover every outcome.
